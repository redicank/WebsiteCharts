Featured Assignment

★  Crown Jewel

Cartography Capstone:
Mapping for University Partners

GEOG 248: Cartography & Spatial Data Visualization
ArcGIS Pro  ·  R  ·  Excel
Partners: Alumni Relations · Annual Giving · Admissions
Running 4+ years

Too often, coursework lives inside a bubble — canned datasets with no real stakes. This project breaks that pattern. Students in Cartography and Spatial Data Visualization use real institutional data to produce professional-grade maps for three university offices that have genuine questions about their own communities: Alumni Relations, Annual Giving, and Admissions.

The data is intentionally simple — alumni by ZIP code, anonymized donor records, prospective students' top program choices — but the messy, real-world nature of it means students must actually clean, pivot, join, and aggregate before they can make a single map. Every group produces a required portfolio of map types (choropleth, bivariate, 3D, dot density), goes through peer critique, and iterates before the final presentation.

How It Works

1
Partners pose questions. Each office brings genuine questions — where do our donors live? Where are prospective students coming from? How does alumni concentration relate to giving rates?
2
Students receive messy data. Raw ZIP-code tables, flat donor exports, and program preference lists require real data wrangling in R or Excel before they can be joined to census geographies.
3
Groups produce a map portfolio. Each team creates choropleth, 3D, bivariate, and dot-density maps across multiple spatial scales, practicing every major visualization type from the course.
4
Peer critique and iteration. Maps go through an in-class feedback round before the final presentation — the same iterative process used by professional cartographers.
5
Public presentation to 15+ guests. On the last day of class, the lab fills with staff from all three offices. Students present their findings and the conversation that follows — about geography, stewardship, and what it means to give back — is often the most memorable part of the course.

Geographically Weighted Regression
GEOG 866 Spatial Data Analysis (Grad) R
▼

A graduate-level lab walking students through multicollinearity testing and global regression before comparing results with geographically weighted statistics — using county-level USGS water usage data to illustrate why spatial non-stationarity matters and when local models outperform global ones.

Amazon Roads Mapping — ABSAT
GEOG 260 Intro GIS ArcGIS Pro · Planet Imagery
▼

Students contribute directly to the Amazon Borderlands Spatial Analysis Team (ABSAT) roads database — manually digitizing illegal logging roads in the Peru-Brazil borderlands from satellite imagery, working in pairs on assigned sections, then merging their work into the shared dataset. The lab has collectively mapped over 20,000 km of roads.

OpenStreetMap for Campus
GEOG 365 Advanced GIS OSM · ArcGIS Pro
▼

Students add and edit at least 20 features in OpenStreetMap using the web editor and a mobile collector app (Vespucci or GoMap!!), then cross-check their partner's contributions for quality. The assignment closes with before-and-after maps showing what they added — connecting students to open-access data infrastructure and community science.

▼

Mortality Spatial Autocorrelation in the South
GEOG 365 Advanced GIS ArcGIS Pro
▼

Students join mortality rate data for nine southern states to county shapefiles and run Getis-Ord GI*, Global Moran's I, and Local Moran's I under different neighborhood definitions — building side-by-side comparison maps and writing critically about which spatial weights are most theoretically appropriate, how edge effects distort results, and what spatial outliers reveal.

Urban Geography Final Project
GEOG 413 Urban Geography R · Census API
▼

Each student selects a unique metropolitan statistical area and writes a research paper connecting one of the semester's core urban theory topics — gentrification, residential segregation, agglomeration economies, political fragmentation — to their chosen city using R and Census API data. Final presentations reveal how the same structural problems surface across wildly different urban contexts, building both technical proficiency and geographic imagination.
